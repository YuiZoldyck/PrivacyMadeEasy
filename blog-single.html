Why Digital Privacy Matters

  • John Doe
  • Jan 1, 2020
  • 12 Comments

Let’s begin by talking about something that all of us understand: privacy in the physical world.

In the physical world, most of us understand what level of personal privacy we want and we generally know how to protect our personal privacy. For example, some people prefer to do the following activities without being seen by others:

  • Using the toilet
  • Getting dressed
  • Participating in a personal spiritual ritual
  • Rehearsing a vocal solo
  • Rehearsing a dance routine

For those who want to do these things in private, the solutions are simple. They can find an enclosed bathroom stall, use a changing room or go to their private living space. Privacy is an innate human value and we will often go to great lengths to protect it. This all might be extremely obvious to you in the physical world, but now I want you to consider your privacy in the digital world.

Imagine yourself at an airport. You’re sitting, as you wait for an upcoming flight, and you decide to take a few minutes to use your smartphone. You look at some cute new pictures of your nieces and nephews, respond to a message from your doctor about your depression medication, and finally, you’re able to take some time to write in your journal about your struggles and victories of the past month. It feels good to get this done and you’re grateful to have an amazing device that allows you to conveniently do all this.


Later, you notice a man standing behind you, looking over your shoulder and directly at your smartphone. You didn’t notice him until now and you’re rather shocked. What would you do in this situation? I typically hear answers like these:

  1. Move far away from this man
  2. Lock your phone and put it away
  3. Tell this man to get lost
  4. Report this man to airport security

One response, I have never encountered, is “Keep using my smartphone as usual and allow this stranger to observe me”.

This makes sense. Again, privacy is an innate human value. Within your smartphone or personal digital space, there are certain activities, conversations, habits, and personal details that you would prefer not to share with others. In some situations, you would like to share such things, but you would only do this with a select group of trusted people. Although people may value privacy in both the physical and digital world, many of them are completely unaware of violations of their privacy in the digital world. The digital world is much more complex. Many of its workings are not visible to the average person.

Now let’s return to that creep at the airport. As I said, most people would not allow this stranger to continue to watch their smartphone activity. Unfortunately, many don’t realize that they are welcoming this digital predator into their lives every day. Malicious individuals, government agencies and private corporations are salivating for your personal data and, in many cases, they successfully suck it right out of you, like a blood-thirsty-mosquito out of an innocent, yet ignorant, rabbit.

Many governments and corporations are determined to collect as much of your personal data as possible. Things such as your purchases, your likes, your locations, your web browsing, your pictures, your habits, your voice, your fears, your health, your messages, your travels, and your phone calls. This data is very valuable to them. Perhaps it is very valuable to you as well. If not, I ask you to consider a new point of view.

Like many average users of the internet, I usually dismissed any digital privacy concerns as paranoid hogwash. However, I had a very informative conversation with a privacy advocate that completely change my perspective. It went something like this:

Me: I know some people have privacy concerns when it comes to massive data collection, but it’s just data. What’s the big deal? It’s just numbers in a computer. Why are some so concerned? It’s not tangible or real.

Privacy Advocate: Well perhaps we can agree that data collection is not inherently good or evil. It is simply a tool. I would point out that it is an enormously valuable tool. If knowledge is power, then big data collection/analysis is God-Like-Power. Think of data as very potent ammunition. Ammunition is also not inherently good or evil. It depends on how it is used. Will your personal data be used to help or protect you? Or will it be used against you? These are important questions and hopefully, those of us who produce the data will ultimately decide if, and how, our personal data is collected and used.

Me: Wait, so you’re saying that massive collection of personal data can be used for good? I thought you were opposed to this entire thing.

Privacy Advocate: Yes, personal data is already used for good purposes and there many future problems that could be solved, using data. By analyzing loads of personal data, we could detect cancers sooner, decrease rates of depression, reduce pollution, save money and save many lives. However, the user needs to understand how and why their data is being collected or analyzed. They should also have some understanding of the risks involved.

Me: What risks are you talking about? You’ve provided these examples of personal data being used for good, but I don’t see any potential downsides.

Privacy Advocate: Well the potential upside of this is massive, but we should also consider some huge potential downsides. Remember big data is like a super-power and, as the saying goes, “Power tends to corrupt, and absolute power corrupts absolutely”. In my view, there are three data-collecting-groups who we should deal with cautiously:

  • Malicious individuals
  • Governments
  • Private companies
